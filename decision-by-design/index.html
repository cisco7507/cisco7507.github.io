Week 1: The lead domino
Why is important to make good decisions?
•
To avoid the consequences of poor decisions in work
•
Save time and grief correcting the mess left by bad decisions
•
Save time for other things we need to move forward
💡
Good decision making compounds over time by making things easier for the future
Where to Start
By the "Lead Domino" which is the decision that is most consequential and irreversible. We start by the addressing the Lead dominoes, since those fall in the most important category i.e They will the bigger and more lasting impact.
When consequential & irreversible decisions are made right, questions like:
•
How do I pay my bills
•
Can I afford this in my future
•
How do I care for my family
Are likely to disappear. Similar to perfectly ordered bottom blocks in Tetris, they simply go away magically. On the contrary if done wrong they compound and accumulate.
💡
Bad decisions also compound overtime making our lives miserable.

Week 2: The Root Problem
Don't get distracted by solving the wrong problem
How we state the problem dictates the solutions we see, and puts us in the path towards success, so before we accept the problem we really need to see if we are solving the right problem.
Most of the time the first problem put on the table is not the problem we need to solve. If we are the decision maker, we need to think through the problem ourselves.
How to solve the right problems
1.
Never accept someone else's definition of the problem. Others can give you a starting point, but we can not jump into solutions.
2.
Put up a firewall between the problem definition and the problem solution.
1.
In teams: Make separate meetings to define the problem and to propose solutions. The tendency is to spend 1 or 2 minutes defining the problem and the rest of the meeting solving it because we are action oriented by nature.
2.
In your personal decisions: Give yourself time to get clear on what the problem is before trying to solve it. Make it concrete
💡
Develop the skill of finding the root problem: When you correctly identify and frame the problem you increase the effectiveness of your decision. The best decision makers separate the process of identifying the problem and addressing the problem. that way the make sure they don't end fixing the symptoms.
Do not end up fixing the symptom(s) caused by the real problem
Ask yourself: What would have to be true for this problem not to exist in the first place.
This is the same question that Lori Weise, the founder of Downtown Dog Rescue in L.A asked herself. She looked at the problem of overcrowded dog shelters and asked: What would have to be true for there to be less dogs to adopt in the first place and a better and permanent solution became clear. She looked into the data and realized that 30% of the dog owners that came to her were "owner surrenders", namely people that would love to keep their pets but couldn't because they simply couldn't afford to feed them. which led to a totally different solution process:

Ask yourself: Will the solution to this particular problem passes the test of time?
A great problem statement contains:
•
What we want
•
Where we want to go
•
What's getting in the way of getting there.
